## Restraint & stripping away (7 canonical)

### [CONCEPT-RESTRAINT-001] Restrain decoration
- **Platform**: Mobile/PC
- **Frequency weight**: 4
- **Sub-category**: Restraint & stripping away

**Principle**: Remove unnecessary visual ornament (glass effects, rounded corners, strokes, etc.). Design exists to communicate information clearly, not to decorate.

**Why**: Ornament is surface; content is substance.

**Anti-pattern**: Applying multiple decorative effects (glass, strokes, gray chips) across many UI elements.

---

### [CONCEPT-RESTRAINT-002] Stripping away is the core
- **Platform**: Mobile/PC
- **Frequency weight**: 3
- **Sub-category**: Restraint & stripping away

**Principle**: Design is more about stripping away than adding. In UX/UI, removing unnecessary elements is the more important skill.

**Why**: Stripping away equals organizing.

**Anti-pattern**: Only adding things in an attempt to decorate.

---

### [CONCEPT-RESTRAINT-003] Minimize effects
- **Platform**: Mobile
- **Frequency weight**: 13
- **Sub-category**: Restraint & stripping away

**Principle**: Use only the minimum effects required. Excessive effects actually damage the focus of the design.

**Why**: Accumulated effects become noise.

**Anti-pattern**: Filling the main billboard with effects.

---

### [CONCEPT-RESTRAINT-004] Use distinctive elements only as accents
- **Platform**: Mobile
- **Frequency weight**: 7
- **Sub-category**: Restraint & stripping away

**Principle**: Distinctive or characterful design elements work best when used as accents. Repeating the same distinctive pattern across every section only increases complexity and erases the effect.

**Why**: Accent impact comes from restraint.

**Anti-pattern**: Repeating a distinctive folder-style design in every corner.

---

### [CONCEPT-RESTRAINT-005] Don't overuse glassmorphism
- **Platform**: Mobile
- **Frequency weight**: 1
- **Sub-category**: Restraint & stripping away

**Principle**: Glassmorphism effects should not be overused without clear intent. In most cases, removing them produces a cleaner design.

**Why**: Effects should appear only when there is intent behind them.

**Anti-pattern**: Applying glass effects in many different places.

---

### [CONCEPT-RESTRAINT-006] Remove complexity first
- **Platform**: Mobile
- **Frequency weight**: 3
- **Sub-category**: Restraint & stripping away

**Principle**: When aiming for minimal design, strip away anything that feels even slightly complex and simplify as much as possible.

**Why**: Complexity violates minimalism.

**Anti-pattern**: Claiming to pursue minimalism while keeping complex elements.

---

### [CONCEPT-RESTRAINT-007] Tidiness equals quality
- **Platform**: Mobile
- **Frequency weight**: 1
- **Sub-category**: Restraint & stripping away

**Principle**: Design quality comes from being well-organized rather than from being unique. Simply organizing elements is enough to reach a solid, normal level.

**Why**: Organizing is design.

**Anti-pattern**: Only trying to make something special.

## Concept consistency (9 canonical)

### [CONCEPT-CONSIST-001] Intent must match outcome
- **Platform**: Mobile/PC
- **Frequency weight**: 10
- **Sub-category**: Concept consistency

**Principle**: The design direction you claim to pursue (e.g., simplicity) must match the actual outcome. Heavy effects and irregular layouts are not simple design.

**Why**: Intent must equal outcome.

**Anti-pattern**: Claiming to pursue simplicity while filling the design with effects.

---

### [CONCEPT-CONSIST-002] Keep the design language consistent
- **Platform**: Mobile/PC
- **Frequency weight**: 5
- **Sub-category**: Concept consistency

**Principle**: Within a single page, the design language (sharp vs. rounded, flat vs. shadowed) must stay consistent. If different styles appear as the user scrolls, unity breaks down.

**Why**: Mixed languages cause distraction.

**Anti-pattern**: Starting sharp at the top, then becoming rounded further down.

---

### [CONCEPT-CONSIST-003] Unify concept across main and sub screens
- **Platform**: Mobile
- **Frequency weight**: 3
- **Sub-category**: Concept consistency

**Principle**: An app's distinctive design concept (layered stacking, style choices, etc.) must be applied consistently across both main and sub screens to create brand unity.

**Why**: Unity is brand.

**Anti-pattern**: Applying the concept to the main screen but ignoring it on sub screens.

---

### [CONCEPT-CONSIST-004] Unity requires consistency in every element
- **Platform**: PC
- **Frequency weight**: 2
- **Sub-category**: Concept consistency

**Principle**: Design unity emerges when the style and visual weight of every element - buttons, fonts, spacing, images - are consistent.

**Why**: A single element alone does not create unity.

**Anti-pattern**: Strong buttons paired with weak fonts, with no balance between them.

---

### [CONCEPT-CONSIST-005] Decide design-language switches up front
- **Platform**: PC
- **Frequency weight**: 2
- **Sub-category**: Concept consistency

**Principle**: Decisions like whether to use rounded corners (border-radius) must be made at the start, in line with the overall concept. Suddenly introducing rounded corners later breaks consistency.

**Why**: Decide from the start.

**Anti-pattern**: Applying rounded corners abruptly partway through.

---

### [CONCEPT-CONSIST-006] Differentiate at the frame level
- **Platform**: Mobile
- **Frequency weight**: 2
- **Sub-category**: Concept consistency

**Principle**: Differentiation should happen at the level of frame, structure, and policy - not in individual details.

**Why**: Tiny differences do not create real differentiation.

**Anti-pattern**: Just swapping the colors on an existing company's style.

---

### [CONCEPT-CONSIST-007] Make the design intent visible
- **Platform**: Mobile/PC
- **Frequency weight**: 1
- **Sub-category**: Concept consistency

**Principle**: The concept and intent of the design must be clearly felt in the final result. If the designer's intent is not visually communicated, the design has failed.

**Why**: Intent must be visible.

**Anti-pattern**: The designer had an intent, but it cannot be seen in the result.

---

### [CONCEPT-CONSIST-008] Don't let concept devour design
- **Platform**: Mobile/PC
- **Frequency weight**: 3
- **Sub-category**: Concept consistency

**Principle**: Design should be the priority, and concept should follow as a supporting layer. Concept should never devour the design.

**Why**: Concept is a supporting role.

**Anti-pattern**: A line-based concept dominates the entire design.

---

### [CONCEPT-CONSIST-009] Commit boldly to a concept
- **Platform**: Mobile/PC
- **Frequency weight**: 1
- **Sub-category**: Concept consistency

**Principle**: Once you commit to a concept, don't go halfway - push it boldly.

**Why**: Half-baked compromises kill the concept's intent and make the design look indecisive.

**Anti-pattern**: Picking a concept but compromising the execution into a half-hearted result.

## Designer growth (18 canonical)

### [CONCEPT-GROW-001] Consider maintainability
- **Platform**: Mobile/PC
- **Frequency weight**: 3
- **Sub-category**: Designer growth

**Principle**: Web design must account for live operation and maintenance, not just visual quality. Without this perspective, the design will not survive in production.

**Why**: Life after launch is longer than before launch.

**Anti-pattern**: Ignoring maintenance entirely.

---

### [CONCEPT-GROW-002] Focus on one area, then expand
- **Platform**: Mobile
- **Frequency weight**: 1
- **Sub-category**: Designer growth

**Principle**: Building skill in a specific area requires focused time on that area. Taking on multiple roles is best done only after reaching a certain level of mastery in one.

**Why**: Depth comes first.

**Anti-pattern**: Starting in multiple disciplines at the same time.

---

### [CONCEPT-GROW-003] Skill over technique
- **Platform**: Mobile/PC
- **Frequency weight**: 2
- **Sub-category**: Designer growth

**Principle**: Portfolio updates should demonstrate growth in design thinking and planning ability, not an increase in surface technique. Technique is not proof of skill.

**Why**: Technique does not equal growth.

**Anti-pattern**: Only improving at front-end publishing skills.

---

### [CONCEPT-GROW-004] Choose and focus
- **Platform**: PC
- **Frequency weight**: 1
- **Sub-category**: Designer growth

**Principle**: When building a portfolio in limited time, choose and focus. Putting energy into your weakest current capability (design or planning) is the right growth strategy.

**Why**: Strategic learning.

**Anti-pattern**: Spreading effort evenly across every area.

---

### [CONCEPT-GROW-005] Publishing < planning and design
- **Platform**: PC
- **Frequency weight**: 1
- **Sub-category**: Designer growth

**Principle**: In portfolio work, cut the time spent on implementation (publishing) and invest in planning and design ability. Time spent on visual effects must not eat into time spent on substance.

**Why**: A portfolio is proof of design ability.

**Anti-pattern**: Spending all the time on front-end publishing.

---

### [CONCEPT-GROW-006] Drop the stubbornness
- **Platform**: Mobile
- **Frequency weight**: 1
- **Sub-category**: Designer growth

**Principle**: Designers need flexibility to accept feedback more than stubbornness about their own style. Repeatedly resubmitting designs with the same problems means missing growth opportunities.

**Why**: Flexibility equals growth.

**Anti-pattern**: Ignoring feedback and resubmitting the same work.

---

### [CONCEPT-GROW-007] Add your own judgment and refine
- **Platform**: Mobile
- **Frequency weight**: 1
- **Sub-category**: Designer growth

**Principle**: Even when using references, you must always refine the work with your own judgment. Only then can you explain your design intent, and only then does your skill improve.

**Why**: Explainability.

**Anti-pattern**: Copying references as-is.

---

### [CONCEPT-GROW-009] Style is the designer's identity
- **Platform**: PC
- **Frequency weight**: 2
- **Sub-category**: Designer growth

**Principle**: A designer needs a consistent style identity. However, when this crosses a line and every output becomes indistinguishable, it becomes a problem.

**Why**: Balance.

**Anti-pattern**: Either getting stuck in one mold, or being completely different every time.

---

### [CONCEPT-GROW-010] Org tone follows the lead
- **Platform**: PC
- **Frequency weight**: 1
- **Sub-category**: Designer growth

**Principle**: An organization's design direction is heavily influenced by the taste and style of its leaders (design lead, senior designer). To change the style, the decision-making structure must change first.

**Why**: Organizational influence.

**Anti-pattern**: A single designer trying to change things alone.

---

### [CONCEPT-GROW-011] Diverse styles require policy
- **Platform**: PC
- **Frequency weight**: 1
- **Sub-category**: Designer growth

**Principle**: Producing genuinely different design styles per project requires design principles, policies, and element definitions to be established first. Without these, the same patterns will keep repeating.

**Why**: Policy enables diversity.

**Anti-pattern**: Working ad hoc with no underlying policy.

---

### [CONCEPT-GROW-012] In-house vs agency
- **Platform**: PC
- **Frequency weight**: 2
- **Sub-category**: Designer growth

**Principle**: In-house designers gain deep domain expertise but may have relatively little experience varying styles. Recognize this trait and compensate through side projects or study.

**Why**: Environment defines your limits.

**Anti-pattern**: Working without realizing the limits of your environment.

---

### [CONCEPT-GROW-013] Each organization has a visual language
- **Platform**: PC
- **Frequency weight**: 1
- **Sub-category**: Designer growth

**Principle**: Strong design organizations have a consistent visual language across their work. This consistency comes from intentional style guides and team culture, and translates directly into brand trust.

**Why**: An organization shows up in its visuals.

**Anti-pattern**: No organizational identity in the work.

---

### [CONCEPT-GROW-014] Renewals are adjustments
- **Platform**: PC
- **Frequency weight**: 1
- **Sub-category**: Designer growth

**Principle**: A website's style guide must be designed carefully on the first build. Renewals tend to be adjustments rather than full replacements, so initial design experience becomes a core competency.

**Why**: The initial build matters most.

**Anti-pattern**: Treating the build lightly because you assume a renewal will fix it later.

---

### [CONCEPT-GROW-015] Evolve familiar styles
- **Platform**: PC
- **Frequency weight**: 1
- **Sub-category**: Designer growth

**Principle**: In-house design tends to evolve with improved refinement and trend awareness while preserving the overall style skeleton. This can be an intentional strategy to preserve user familiarity.

**Why**: User familiarity.

**Anti-pattern**: Suddenly switching to something completely new.

---

### [CONCEPT-GROW-016] Study current trends
- **Platform**: Mobile
- **Frequency weight**: 1
- **Sub-category**: Designer growth

**Principle**: Stop repeatedly referring to older designs - keep watching how things are being done today.

**Why**: Trends and patterns shift quickly, so relying only on old references makes the work feel dated.

**Anti-pattern**: Referring only to work from years ago and producing results that feel out of touch.

---

### [CONCEPT-GROW-017] Before/After comparison
- **Platform**: Mobile/PC
- **Frequency weight**: 1
- **Sub-category**: Designer growth

**Principle**: Show before/after comparisons to other people and judge quality based on their reactions.

**Why**: Your own perspective is limited; outside reactions are a fast way to validate your work.

**Anti-pattern**: Finishing based on your own judgment alone and shipping without any validation.

---

### [CONCEPT-GROW-018] Fundamentals come first
- **Platform**: Mobile/PC
- **Frequency weight**: 2
- **Sub-category**: Designer growth

**Principle**: Fundamentals matter far more than fancy design - you really need to practice them a lot.

**Why**: Weak fundamentals make even flashy work look awkward and cap your overall skill.

**Anti-pattern**: Skipping the fundamentals and jumping straight to flashy expression, producing awkward and amateurish results.

---

### [CONCEPT-GROW-019] Practice web design too
- **Platform**: PC
- **Frequency weight**: 1
- **Sub-category**: Designer growth

**Principle**: Don't only get good at mobile - make sure to practice web design too. Strong web designers are rare.

**Why**: Web design has different information density and layout complexity, so it needs separate practice.

**Anti-pattern**: A designer who is comfortable only with mobile and clumsy with web screens.

## Usability first (14 canonical)

### [CONCEPT-USAB-001] Usability is the top priority
- **Platform**: Mobile
- **Frequency weight**: 2
- **Sub-category**: Usability first

**Principle**: In UX/UI design, usability outranks visual appeal. Readability matters far more here than in other design disciplines.

**Why**: The essence of UX.

**Anti-pattern**: Focusing only on looking pretty.

---

### [CONCEPT-USAB-002] User-centered design
- **Platform**: Mobile
- **Frequency weight**: 3
- **Sub-category**: Usability first

**Principle**: UI design must always weigh user convenience alongside visual quality.

**Why**: The user is the subject of the design.

**Anti-pattern**: Visual design that ignores the user.

---

### [CONCEPT-USAB-003] Readability over prettiness
- **Platform**: Mobile
- **Frequency weight**: 1
- **Sub-category**: Usability first

**Principle**: Modern UX design must prioritize user convenience and intuitive interaction over aesthetic originality.

**Why**: Current trend.

**Anti-pattern**: Making it pretty but inconvenient to use.

---

### [CONCEPT-USAB-004] Engineering, not illustration
- **Platform**: Mobile
- **Frequency weight**: 1
- **Sub-category**: Usability first

**Principle**: UI design is engineering grounded in usability, not graphic art. Visual expression must stay within the bounds of usability.

**Why**: UI is a tool.

**Anti-pattern**: Approaching UI as if it were a painting.

---

### [CONCEPT-USAB-005] Convenience over uniqueness
- **Platform**: Mobile
- **Frequency weight**: 1
- **Sub-category**: Usability first

**Principle**: The core question in design should be 'how do I improve readability,' not 'how do I look unique.'

**Why**: Readability is the essence.

**Anti-pattern**: Obsessing over uniqueness.

---

### [CONCEPT-USAB-006] Prefer familiar patterns
- **Platform**: Mobile/PC
- **Frequency weight**: 1
- **Sub-category**: Usability first

**Principle**: New UI patterns are not always better. Improving familiar patterns to be slightly more convenient is more effective from a UX perspective.

**Why**: Learning cost.

**Anti-pattern**: Believing that new UI is always better.

---

### [CONCEPT-USAB-007] Usability first, concept second
- **Platform**: PC
- **Frequency weight**: 1
- **Sub-category**: Usability first

**Principle**: When benchmarking concept-driven designs, consider usability first and apply the concept on top. If concept beats usability, the result is unusable in production.

**Why**: Concept does not justify sacrificing usability.

**Anti-pattern**: Obsessing over the concept alone.

---

### [CONCEPT-USAB-008] Information pages serve information
- **Platform**: Mobile
- **Frequency weight**: 1
- **Sub-category**: Usability first

**Principle**: On screens where conveying information is the point (order forms, receipts, settings), prioritize readability and information clarity over visual flair.

**Why**: Information is the top priority.

**Anti-pattern**: Trying to dress up an order form to look pretty.

---

### [CONCEPT-USAB-009] Understand the domain before designing
- **Platform**: Mobile
- **Frequency weight**: 1
- **Sub-category**: Usability first

**Principle**: Before designing a critical page, deeply understand its business and user purpose first. Sensitive screens like checkout demand information accuracy and process clarity above all.

**Why**: Context understanding.

**Anti-pattern**: Designing without knowing what the screen is for.

---

### [CONCEPT-USAB-010] No decoration on functional pages
- **Platform**: Mobile
- **Frequency weight**: 4
- **Sub-category**: Usability first

**Principle**: On function-driven pages like login, the top priority is intuitive, clear flow that lets the user complete the task - not visual flair.

**Why**: Function is the purpose.

**Anti-pattern**: Loud gradients on a login page.

---

### [CONCEPT-USAB-011] Keep functional pages light
- **Platform**: Mobile
- **Frequency weight**: 2
- **Sub-category**: Usability first

**Principle**: For function-focused list and search pages, a light, intuitive design fits better than heavy styling.

**Why**: Function calls for lightness.

**Anti-pattern**: Making a search results screen overly stylish.

---

### [CONCEPT-USAB-012] Improve the familiar over inventing the new
- **Platform**: Mobile/PC
- **Frequency weight**: 1
- **Sub-category**: Usability first

**Principle**: Commerce product detail pages should prioritize information readability, grouping, ease of use, and familiarity over originality. Improving a familiar pattern is better than inventing new UI.

**Why**: Ease of use.

**Anti-pattern**: Trying to make it pretty above all else.

---

### [CONCEPT-USAB-013] Question whether customers can actually use it
- **Platform**: Mobile
- **Frequency weight**: 1
- **Sub-category**: Usability first

**Principle**: The basic premise of design is to constantly ask: 'Can a customer actually use this?'

**Why**: The baseline mindset for every decision must be real-user feasibility, not designer satisfaction.

**Anti-pattern**: Building only against your own designer-tier standards with no user validation.

---

### [CONCEPT-USAB-014] Focus on the content
- **Platform**: Mobile/PC
- **Frequency weight**: 1
- **Sub-category**: Usability first

**Principle**: Portfolio and content pages must focus on the content itself, not the design.

**Why**: The moment you focus on design, the actual content delivery goes off the rails and you lose the substance.

**Anti-pattern**: A portfolio whose decorative design upstages the actual content.

## Benchmarking (10 canonical)

### [CONCEPT-BENCH-001] Validate benchmark sources
- **Platform**: Mobile
- **Frequency weight**: 2
- **Sub-category**: Benchmarking

**Principle**: Before benchmarking, distinguish whether the source is actually UI design or editorial design, then choose accordingly.

**Why**: Wrong source produces wrong results.

**Anti-pattern**: Applying poster design language to a mobile app.

---

### [CONCEPT-BENCH-002] Reference only realistic sources
- **Platform**: Mobile
- **Frequency weight**: 3
- **Sub-category**: Benchmarking

**Principle**: Designs you benchmark must be realistic ones running in real services. Copying conceptual portfolio pieces or unrealistic references results in work that does not survive in production.

**Why**: Reality is not the same as concept work.

**Anti-pattern**: Copying impractical Dribbble designs.

---

### [CONCEPT-BENCH-003] Borrow only the feeling
- **Platform**: Mobile
- **Frequency weight**: 1
- **Sub-category**: Benchmarking

**Principle**: From visual references, take only the style, mood, and concept. Redesign the actual UI implementation based on your own judgment and platform guidelines.

**Why**: Referring is not copying.

**Anti-pattern**: Tracing Dribbble work directly.

---

### [CONCEPT-BENCH-005] Benchmark with real measurements
- **Platform**: Mobile
- **Frequency weight**: 1
- **Sub-category**: Benchmarking

**Principle**: Design values (font size, spacing, button height, etc.) should be reference-checked by actually measuring the benchmark app. Build the habit of designing on numbers, not eyeballs.

**Why**: Numbers bring precision.

**Anti-pattern**: Eyeballing it to make it 'kinda look like' the reference.

---

### [CONCEPT-BENCH-006] Don't blindly follow famous companies
- **Platform**: PC
- **Frequency weight**: 1
- **Sub-category**: Benchmarking

**Principle**: Do not blindly follow the design patterns of famous companies. Judge first whether they fit your own standards and context. The choices of big companies are not always best.

**Why**: The context is different.

**Anti-pattern**: Mimicking IKEA just because they did it.

---

### [CONCEPT-BENCH-007] Reference app values belong to that app's context
- **Platform**: Mobile
- **Frequency weight**: 3
- **Sub-category**: Benchmarking

**Principle**: A reference app's UI values were set inside that app's brand context. Don't simply copy them - re-evaluate them inside your own project's context.

**Why**: Context-dependent.

**Anti-pattern**: 'Instagram does it that way, so we should too.'

---

### [CONCEPT-BENCH-008] Reference many apps broadly
- **Platform**: Mobile
- **Frequency weight**: 2
- **Sub-category**: Benchmarking

**Principle**: To create a solid, normal design, benchmark broadly across many apps and iterate on details. Referencing only one app produces biased results.

**Why**: Variety brings robustness.

**Anti-pattern**: Referencing only a single app.

---

### [CONCEPT-BENCH-009] Benchmark UX too, not just visuals
- **Platform**: Mobile/PC
- **Frequency weight**: 1
- **Sub-category**: Benchmarking

**Principle**: Benchmarking must analyze UX flow, information structure, element placement, and interaction patterns alongside visual style. Copying only the visuals produces a design with no UX.

**Why**: The whole is the design.

**Anti-pattern**: Copying only the visuals.

---

### [CONCEPT-BENCH-010] Distinguish portfolio sites from live services
- **Platform**: PC
- **Frequency weight**: 2
- **Sub-category**: Benchmarking

**Principle**: UI design benchmarking should treat portfolio sites (Pinterest, Dribbble, etc.) and live operating services differently. Look to currently running services for design policy, element composition, and information structure.

**Why**: Portfolios are for visuals; live services are for policy.

**Anti-pattern**: Pulling design policy from Dribbble alone.

---

### [CONCEPT-BENCH-012] Copy-learning is fine for beginners
- **Platform**: Mobile
- **Frequency weight**: 1
- **Sub-category**: Benchmarking

**Principle**: Tracing references is a valid way to learn basic intuition for font size, spacing, and color. After this stage, however, you must move on to designs that include your own judgment.

**Why**: Stage-based learning.

**Anti-pattern**: Shipping copy-learning practice as production design.

## System & standards first (8 canonical)

### [CONCEPT-SYS-001] Design system first
- **Platform**: Mobile/PC
- **Frequency weight**: 4
- **Sub-category**: System & standards first

**Principle**: From the very first practice stage, build the habit of designing on top of shared components and a style system. Bad habits become maintenance debt in production.

**Why**: Systems are the foundation of growth.

**Anti-pattern**: Designing each screen as an independent one-off.

---

### [CONCEPT-SYS-003] Design workflow order
- **Platform**: Mobile/PC
- **Frequency weight**: 2
- **Sub-category**: System & standards first

**Principle**: The design order is always: (1) guides (regions, alignment, spacing) -> (2) usability and structure -> (3) visual effects. Decorating before the underlying structure exists never solves the root issue.

**Why**: Skeleton before finish.

**Anti-pattern**: Starting with decoration.

---

### [CONCEPT-SYS-004] Skeleton first, seasoning second
- **Platform**: Mobile
- **Frequency weight**: 1
- **Sub-category**: System & standards first

**Principle**: In UI design, visual effects (color, graphics, gradients) are seasoning. The skeleton (structure, spacing, type system) must be finished before visual layers go on top.

**Why**: The metaphor brings clarity.

**Anti-pattern**: Sprinkling seasoning before there's a dish.

---

### [CONCEPT-SYS-005] Design is a system
- **Platform**: Mobile
- **Frequency weight**: 1
- **Sub-category**: System & standards first

**Principle**: Design operates as a system. Changing one element requires adjusting the related ones. This is the normal design process, and it requires systems thinking.

**Why**: Interconnectedness.

**Anti-pattern**: Changing one element and calling it done.

---

### [CONCEPT-SYS-006] Have your own clear standards
- **Platform**: Mobile/PC
- **Frequency weight**: 5
- **Sub-category**: System & standards first

**Principle**: A designer must hold clear personal design standards. Without them, you get pushed around by external references and the design loses consistency and becomes a mess.

**Why**: Standards create consistency.

**Anti-pattern**: Changing the design every time you look at it.

---

### [CONCEPT-SYS-008] Consistent standards make a system
- **Platform**: PC
- **Frequency weight**: 2
- **Sub-category**: System & standards first

**Principle**: Design must have consistent standards (ratios, font sizes, spacing). Consistency of those standards outranks aesthetic polish.

**Why**: Consistency is the system.

**Anti-pattern**: Wildly uneven values with no underlying standard.

---

### [CONCEPT-SYS-009] Fast projects need a system
- **Platform**: PC
- **Frequency weight**: 1
- **Sub-category**: System & standards first

**Principle**: On fast project cycles, redesigning the underlying logic from scratch each time is impractical. Pre-built reusable design systems and principles secure both speed and quality.

**Why**: Reuse equals efficiency.

**Anti-pattern**: Starting from scratch every time.

---

### [CONCEPT-SYS-002] Structure first, details later
- **Platform**: Mobile/PC
- **Frequency weight**: 1
- **Sub-category**: System & standards first

**Principle**: Lock down the frame and structure first, then handle details - if the frame is shaky, details have no meaning.

**Why**: Building details before structure is fixed breaks consistency and explodes the amount of rework needed.

**Anti-pattern**: Drawing details before settling the frame, ending up having to rebuild the whole thing later.

## Minimalism vs decoration (8 canonical)

### [CONCEPT-MIN-001] Restrain decoration in minimal design
- **Platform**: PC
- **Frequency weight**: 2
- **Sub-category**: Minimalism vs decoration

**Principle**: When pursuing minimal design, minimize ornamental elements like rounded corners and shadows, or use them sparingly with clear intent. Otherwise, the concept loses consistency.

**Why**: Minimalism is emptying.

**Anti-pattern**: Calling it minimal while using rounded corners and shadows.

---

### [CONCEPT-MIN-002] Accept the plainness of minimal
- **Platform**: Mobile/PC
- **Frequency weight**: 1
- **Sub-category**: Minimalism vs decoration

**Principle**: If you're aiming for minimal design, you have to accept that it will look plain. Adding decoration out of insecurity damages the concept.

**Why**: Plainness is the essence of minimalism.

**Anti-pattern**: Adding decoration just because it feels too plain.

---

### [CONCEPT-MIN-003] Two directions of minimal
- **Platform**: PC
- **Frequency weight**: 1
- **Sub-category**: Minimalism vs decoration

**Principle**: Minimal design has two directions: emptying by removing elements, or organizing many elements through alignment and structure. Either way, the result must feel deliberately organized.

**Why**: Organized feeling is the core.

**Anti-pattern**: Just emptying things out without organizing.

---

### [CONCEPT-MIN-004] Minimal is not hiding
- **Platform**: Mobile
- **Frequency weight**: 1
- **Sub-category**: Minimalism vs decoration

**Principle**: Minimalism is not about hiding elements or shrinking them. It is maximized when whitespace, contrast, and hierarchy are tuned properly and elements are placed thoughtfully.

**Why**: Get the definition right.

**Anti-pattern**: Faking minimalism by making everything small or faded.

---

### [CONCEPT-MIN-005] Three principles for finishing minimal design
- **Platform**: PC
- **Frequency weight**: 1
- **Sub-category**: Minimalism vs decoration

**Principle**: Finishing a minimal design requires three considerations together: stripping away unnecessary elements, actively using whitespace, and balancing the overall sense of volume.

**Why**: Balance across three axes.

**Anti-pattern**: Only stripping things away without considering whitespace.

---

### [CONCEPT-MIN-006] Minimalism depends on domain fit
- **Platform**: Mobile/PC
- **Frequency weight**: 2
- **Sub-category**: Minimalism vs decoration

## Intentional design (6 canonical)

### [CONCEPT-RATIONAL-001] Every element needs a reason
- **Platform**: Mobile/PC
- **Frequency weight**: 3
- **Sub-category**: Intentional design

**Principle**: Every design decision needs a reason. If you cannot explain why an element belongs where it is, remove it.

**Why**: Reasons are justification.

**Anti-pattern**: Adding things just because they look pretty.

---

### [CONCEPT-RATIONAL-002] 'It's pretty' is not a reason
- **Platform**: PC
- **Frequency weight**: 1
- **Sub-category**: Intentional design

**Principle**: Every design element needs a clear reason. 'It's pretty' or 'it just looks good' are not convincing reasons. Even when no reason exists, create one to develop your own standards.

**Why**: Subjectivity is not persuasion.

**Anti-pattern**: Using 'it's pretty' as the actual justification.

---

### [CONCEPT-RATIONAL-003] No reason, no element
- **Platform**: PC
- **Frequency weight**: 2
- **Sub-category**: Intentional design

**Principle**: When adding a design element, always check for the reason first. If there is no reason, it's better not to add it. Simple beats complex.

**Why**: Simple is safe.

**Anti-pattern**: Adding elements without a reason.

---

### [CONCEPT-RATIONAL-004] Reasons for lines and strokes
- **Platform**: Mobile
- **Frequency weight**: 1
- **Sub-category**: Intentional design

**Principle**: Visual boundary elements like strokes and dividers should be used because 'separation isn't readable,' not because 'it looks pretty.' Every design element needs a clear functional reason.

**Why**: Function is the reason.

**Anti-pattern**: Adding lines because they look nice.

---

### [CONCEPT-RATIONAL-005] Complex or distracting? Remove it
- **Platform**: Mobile
- **Frequency weight**: 1
- **Sub-category**: Intentional design

**Principle**: Each design element needs a reason to exist. If an element could be removed for being 'too complex, too distracting, or focus-killing,' don't add it in the first place.

**Why**: Vet it up front.

**Anti-pattern**: Repeatedly adding elements and then removing them because they get too complex.

---

### [CONCEPT-RATIONAL-006] The essence of being a designer
- **Platform**: Mobile
- **Frequency weight**: 1
- **Sub-category**: Intentional design

**Principle**: The essence of design is not the visual output but the process of diagnosing and solving user problems.

**Why**: The process is the essence.

**Anti-pattern**: Focusing only on the final visual output.

## Composition first (7 canonical)

### [CONCEPT-COMP-001] Composition before visuals
- **Platform**: Mobile/PC
- **Frequency weight**: 1
- **Sub-category**: Composition first

**Principle**: On information-heavy pages, finish composition and information placement first, then add visual elements only minimally. Leading with visuals destroys the information structure.

**Why**: Composition is the skeleton.

**Anti-pattern**: Starting with visual elements.

---

### [CONCEPT-COMP-002] After composition, add visuals lightly
- **Platform**: Mobile/PC
- **Frequency weight**: 1
- **Sub-category**: Composition first

**Principle**: Add visual elements only lightly, after the composition is complete and only when it feels too plain. Heavy visual touches make the carefully thought-out composition meaningless.

**Why**: Protect the composition.

**Anti-pattern**: Adding strong visual touches once composition is done.

---

### [CONCEPT-COMP-003] Don't over-decorate after composition
- **Platform**: Mobile/PC
- **Frequency weight**: 1
- **Sub-category**: Composition first

**Principle**: Once composition is set, piling on shapes, colors, and effects strips the layout composition of meaning. Visual elements must stay at a level that supports the composition.

**Why**: Supporting is not overpowering.

**Anti-pattern**: Excessive shapes, colors, and effects piled on after composition.

---

### [CONCEPT-COMP-004] Design with real content in mind
- **Platform**: Mobile
- **Frequency weight**: 2
- **Sub-category**: Composition first

**Principle**: Design is not drawing - it is building structures that hold content. Always check how things look once real content (text, images) is loaded in.

**Why**: Content is the subject of design.

**Anti-pattern**: Designing only with dummy content.

---

### [CONCEPT-COMP-005] Don't decorate without basic guides
- **Platform**: Mobile
- **Frequency weight**: 1
- **Sub-category**: Composition first

**Principle**: Adding visual decoration before the underlying structure (guides) is in place actually hides the problems. Decoration always comes after structure is finished.

**Why**: Structure first.

**Anti-pattern**: Decorating without any guides in place.

---

### [CONCEPT-COMP-007] Always account for fixed elements
- **Platform**: Mobile
- **Frequency weight**: 1
- **Sub-category**: Composition first

**Principle**: When designing, always consider the entire on-screen state the user actually experiences - including fixed bars and overlays - not just a single isolated screen.

**Why**: Whole-context view.

**Anti-pattern**: Looking only at the screen you're currently editing.

---

### [CONCEPT-COMP-008] Fill empty regions with substance
- **Platform**: Mobile/PC
- **Frequency weight**: 1
- **Sub-category**: Composition first

**Principle**: When there's so much whitespace that the page looks empty, fill it with content like charts, tables, or checklists.

**Why**: Thin content turns whitespace into meaningless empty space and lowers the page's value.

**Anti-pattern**: A screen with nothing but large empty spaces and no information.
